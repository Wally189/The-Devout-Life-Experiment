Before practical work

Come, Holy Spirit

Come, Holy Spirit, fill the hearts of your faithful and kindle in them the fire of your love. Send forth your Spirit and they shall be created, and you shall renew the face of the earth.

O God, who by the light of the Holy Spirit did instruct the hearts of the faithful, grant that by the same Holy Spirit we may be truly wise and ever rejoice in his consolations. Through Christ our Lord. Amen.

Remain silent for a moment and prepare yourself gently for the work.

The practical track

Use this independently or alongside the weekly spiritual lesson. Its purpose is not to manage every detail of life. It helps bring one real matter at a time into truth, action, review and peace.

Set up one notebook

  • Leave the first two pages blank for an index.
  • Use the front for spiritual reading, examinations and weekly reviews.
  • Start from the back for the Practical Register, action pages, food plans, budgets and later Rule of Life drafts.
  • Date entries and number pages.
  • Use ordinary ink for facts, another colour for actions or dates, and a third only for completion or important insight. Pencil may hold provisional ideas.

The colours are optional. Do not delay beginning in order to buy ideal stationery.

Step one

Capture, then narrow

On a double-page spread, create columns for: Matter · Type · Urgency · Next physical action · Date · Status.

Use □ not started · ◐ in progress · ✓ complete · → scheduled · ? help needed · × no longer required.

After the first capture, circle no more than three urgent matters and three matters for this week. Schedule or defer the rest. The register stores the whole field; today should reveal only a small working set.

Examples if you feel stuck

Replace “sort money” with “open each account and record the balance.” Replace “fix the house” with “clear and clean the kitchen worktops.” Replace “deal with health” with “telephone the GP at 8:00 on Monday.”

Step two

Choose the next faithful thing

  1. Faith and moral duty: serious sin, confession, restitution, promises and justice.
  2. Safety and health: urgent care, dependency, sleep, food, medication and risk.
  3. Fixed commitments: bills, appointments, employment, travel and duties owed to others.
  4. Basic domestic function: bed, laundry, bathroom, kitchen, food and waste.
  5. Future protection: safeguards, budgets, meal plans, calendars and preparation.
  6. Improvement and beauty: deeper cleaning, garden, projects and optional refinement.
Duty before optimisation. Function before beauty. Repair before expansion.
Step three

Use an Action Page only when needed

Ordinary tasks do not require elaborate analysis. Use a full page when the matter has several steps, remains unclear, carries strong emotion, involves another person, concerns health or money, or repeatedly returns unresolved.

  1. What is the observable fact?
  2. What am I assuming, fearing or attributing to another person?
  3. What part is mine?
  4. What is outside my control?
  5. What kind of matter is this?
  6. What would sufficiently resolved look like?
  7. What is the next physical action?
  8. When will I do it, and what help or material is needed?
  9. What happened after action?
  10. Decision: complete, continue, schedule, seek help or defer deliberately.
Gentle sentence starters

“The plain fact is…” · “My responsibility is…” · “I cannot decide another person’s response…” · “A sufficient next step is…” · “I do not yet know; I need more evidence or counsel.”

Daily use

A suggested task list

Choose the anchors and only two or three supporting items. This is not a private law.

Anchors

  • □ Keep the appointed prayer time or times.
  • □ Complete the principal duty of work, study, care or recovery.
  • □ End with a brief review and night prayer.

Choose two or three supports

  • □ One next action from the register.
  • □ One necessary domestic duty.
  • □ Follow an existing food or spending plan.
  • □ Appropriate movement, rest or recreation.
  • □ Prepare tomorrow.
  • □ Optional daily Mass readings.

At night write three lines: What was completed? What remains and now has a date? What needs to be redirected?

Test before promising

Principles for an ordered lay life

Prayer gives shape

Fixed points of prayer interrupt self-absorption and appetite.

Stability

Remain faithful to sound duties and ordinary places rather than continually escaping into novelty.

Work as service

Employment, care, domestic labour and administration can be offered peacefully for others.

Moderation

Food, fasting, sleep, study and projects must suit health and vocation.

Silence and measured speech

Create space before speaking, messaging or reacting.

Stewardship

Care for time, money, food, body, home and possessions as things entrusted to you.

Hospitality and charity

Order should make room for legitimate need and human relationship.

Rest and recreation

Rest is part of a human life, not a reward for perfection.

Only when ready

Discern a provisional Rule of Life

Do not write one on the first day and do not rewrite it daily. After enough observation—perhaps several weeks—retain only practices that have proved useful, charitable and sustainable.

Write one or two pages titled Provisional Rule of Life — Version 1. Date it and set a review date about four weeks later.

  • Purpose and whom the rule helps you serve.
  • Prayer and sacraments.
  • Rising, sleep and bodily care.
  • Work, travel and study.
  • Food, fasting and money.
  • Home, possessions and maintenance.
  • Speech, digital conduct and relationships.
  • Service, recreation and rest.
  • Weekly and monthly review.

Exception clause: illness, necessary care of another person, travel, legitimate work demands and works of charity may alter the pattern without constituting failure.

Recovery clause: when the rule breaks down, resume the next fixed prayer, restore food, sleep and essential duties, choose one practical action, and wait for stability before redesigning anything.

Weekly discernment

  1. What consistently helped prayer, duty, health and charity?
  2. What repeatedly failed, and was the cause unwillingness, poor timing, excessive scope, health or missing support?
  3. What should be retained, reduced, moved, removed or tested longer?
  4. What requires confession, spiritual counsel, healthcare, debt advice or practical assistance?
At the end

Lord, receive the work I have done and the work I could not complete. Give me grace to do the next duty faithfully, patience with what remains unresolved, and charity toward those whose lives touch mine. Keep me from haste, severity and discouragement. Through Christ our Lord. Amen.

Do not restart. Resume. One action, one day and one review are enough to begin again.
