Companion to the twelve-week reading course

The Practical Rule

This page does not store private answers. It directs you to use your paper journal: list what needs attention, decide what is truly required, tick off completed actions, review what remains, and gradually write a modest Rule of Life suited to a Catholic layperson.

Use the journal, not the screen. Keep sins, debts, health details, relationship matters and private failures in your own notebook or secure record. The website supplies the method, headings and order.
Stage one

Clear immediate disorder

List urgent matters, choose the next physical actions and complete or contain them before beginning elaborate reforms.

Stage two

Establish a rhythm

Fix prayer, rising, work, study, meals, domestic care, recreation and sleep into a repeatable weekly pattern.

Stage three

Write a Rule of Life

Retain only practices proved useful and sustainable. The Rule should serve vocation and charity, not become a private monastery or a test of personal severity.

Journal exercise one

Create the Practical Register

Open a fresh double-page spread. Write each matter on a separate line and leave room to update it.

Copy these column headings

  1. Matter. One plain sentence.
  2. Type. Spiritual, health, money, domestic, work, relationship, administration, project or uncertain.
  3. Urgency. Today, this week, before work begins, scheduled later, or deliberately deferred.
  4. Next physical action. Something visible that can actually be done.
  5. Date. When it will be attempted.
  6. Status. Use □ not started, ◐ in progress, ✓ completed, → scheduled, ? help needed, or × no longer required.

Do not write “sort finances” or “fix the house.” Write “record every account balance” or “clear and clean the kitchen worktops.”

How to choose what comes first

The order of attention

Use this order when several matters compete for attention.

  1. Faith and moral duty. Serious sin, confession, restitution, promises and obligations of justice.
  2. Safety and health. Matters involving medical risk, dependency, sleep, food, medication or urgent care.
  3. Fixed deadlines. Bills, appointments, employment documents, travel and commitments to others.
  4. Basic domestic function. Bed, laundry, bathroom, kitchen, food and waste before decorative improvements.
  5. Future protection. Safeguards, routines, budgets, meal plans and work preparation.
  6. Improvement and beauty. Garden, deeper cleaning, projects and optional refinements.

Discern before acting

In the journal, answer: What is the observable fact? What part is mine? What does justice or charity require? What is outside my control? What remedy fits the kind of problem? What would be sufficient rather than perfect?

Journal exercise two

Use one Action Page per important matter

Copy these headings by hand. Do not answer them on the website.

  1. What is happening? State facts, dates, amounts, frequency or visible conditions.
  2. What am I assuming? Separate interpretation, fear and imagined motive from evidence.
  3. What is my responsibility? Name sin, neglect, duty or practical ownership without claiming control over other people.
  4. What kind of matter is this? Sin, habit, near occasion, health matter, wound, finance, domestic duty, relationship, work, project or uncertainty.
  5. What would sufficiently resolved look like? Define the minimum stable condition.
  6. What is the next physical action?
  7. When will I do it?
  8. What help, cost, material or journey is required?
  9. What happened? Record evidence after acting.
  10. Decision. Tick one: □ complete □ continue □ schedule □ seek help □ defer deliberately.
A lay use of monastic wisdom

Principles for a Rule of Life

These are translations of enduring monastic principles into ordinary lay life. They are not monastic vows.

Prayer gives the day its shape

Keep fixed points of prayer so work and appetite do not occupy the whole day. Your present pattern is morning, evening and night prayer; Angelus at noon and 6:00 pm; and one daily Rosary.

Stability

Remain faithful to sound duties and ordinary places instead of escaping continually into novelty. Finish the next proper task before inventing another system.

Work as service

Domestic labour, employment and administration are not interruptions to spiritual life. Do them carefully, peacefully and for the good of others.

Moderation

Food, fasting, sleep, recreation, study and projects should be proportionate to health and vocation. Severity is not automatically holiness.

Silence and measured speech

Create space before speaking, messaging or reacting. Silence serves listening, recollection and charity; it is not withdrawal or punishment.

Stewardship

Care for money, possessions, food, time, body, home and garden as things entrusted to you rather than raw material for impulse or neglect.

Hospitality and charity

A rule that makes you less available to legitimate need is defective. Order should make room for Mass, family, friendship, service and the unexpected person.

Obedience to legitimate duty

For a layperson this means fidelity to God, Church teaching, lawful authority, employment duties, promises and the real responsibilities of one’s state in life.

Conversion and review

Expect correction. Review the rule weekly and revise calmly where it proves unrealistic, indulgent, unsafe or uncharitable.

Recreation and rest

Rest is part of a human rule, not a reward for perfection. Plan free or affordable pleasure that restores rather than disrupts sleep, money or duty.

Journal exercise three

Draft the first Rule of Life

After the fortnight, use one page only. Write commitments under the headings below.

  1. Purpose. One sentence explaining whom the rule is meant to help you serve.
  2. Prayer. Daily, weekly and sacramental commitments.
  3. Rising and sleep. Normal times and the evening shutdown.
  4. Work and commute. Duties, preparation, travel and the Latin/Spanish practice block.
  5. Food and fasting. Meal planning, Friday discipline and health safeguards.
  6. Home and possessions. Daily reset, weekly cleaning, laundry, garden and maintenance.
  7. Money. Budgeting, purchasing delays, bills and review.
  8. Speech and digital conduct. Boundaries, silence and delayed responses.
  9. Relationships and service. Family, friendship, parish and hospitality.
  10. Study and projects. Limits, priorities and how experiments are tested.
  11. Recreation. Planned free or affordable enjoyment.
  12. Review. A fixed weekly review and a larger monthly review.

Use “ordinarily” rather than “always” where illness, charity, travel or legitimate duty may require adaptation.

Daily journal checklist

Write, act and tick off

At the start of each day, copy only the items needed for that day.

  • □ Rise at the appointed time.
  • □ Morning prayer.
  • □ Latin and Spanish commute practice, or use an actual journey.
  • □ One spiritual or moral action.
  • □ One urgent practical action.
  • □ One domestic action.
  • □ One future-protection action.
  • □ Noon Angelus.
  • □ Planned food rather than improvised spending.
  • □ Planned free or low-cost recreation.
  • □ 6:00 pm Angelus and evening prayer.
  • □ Rosary in the day’s language.
  • □ Prepare tomorrow.
  • □ Night prayer and brief examination.

At night write three lines: What was completed? What remains and has a date? What must be redirected tomorrow?

Weekly review

Do not merely accumulate ticks

A checklist measures completion; discernment asks whether the rule is producing charity, peace and fidelity.

  1. Which duties were consistently completed?
  2. Which commitments repeatedly failed, and why?
  3. Was the problem unwillingness, poor timing, excessive scope, health, missing materials or an unrealistic rule?
  4. Did the rule improve prayer, work, home, health and relationships?
  5. Did it produce agitation, pride, rigidity or judgement of others?
  6. What one practice should be retained?
  7. What one practice should be reduced, moved or removed?
  8. What requires confession, counsel, healthcare or practical assistance?
Final safeguard: a lay Rule of Life must remain subordinate to the commandments, the Church, health, charity and the duties of your actual vocation. It is a servant of life with God, not a private law by which to condemn yourself.
