Companion to the twelve-week reading course

The Practical Work

This page teaches a private paper method for bringing duties, problems and plans into order. First record reality, act upon it, test a rhythm and only later retain what has proved sound in a provisional Rule of Life.

The sequence matters: set up the journal → create the register → choose daily actions → review the evidence → test a rhythm → discern a provisional Rule of Life.
Begin here

Set up the journal

One ordinary ruled notebook is enough. This arrangement keeps spiritual reflection and practical administration distinct while allowing each to inform the other.

Use the front for spiritual work

Begin at the first page and work forwards. Date every entry. Use this side for lesson notes, summaries from memory, examinations, prayers, amendments and Sunday reviews.

Use the back for practical work

Turn the notebook over and begin from the final page, working backwards. Use this side for the Practical Register, action pages, weekly task lists, food plans, budgets and later drafts of the Rule of Life.

A simple marking system

  • Black or blue: ordinary writing and facts.
  • Red: correction, warning, deadline or matter requiring attention—not a colour for condemning yourself.
  • Green: completed action, grace received, improvement or something to retain.
  • Pencil: provisional plans, questions and wording likely to change.

Different colours are optional. Consistency matters more than stationery. Add page numbers as you go and create a contents page only after enough material exists to need one.

Journal exercise one

Create the Practical Register

Open a double-page spread at the back. Write each matter on one line and leave room for later changes.

Copy these headings

  1. Matter: one plain sentence.
  2. Type: spiritual, health, money, domestic, work, relationship, administration, project or uncertain.
  3. Urgency: today, this week, fixed date, scheduled later or deliberately deferred.
  4. Next physical action: something visible that can be done.
  5. Date: when it will be attempted.
  6. Status: □ not started · ◐ in progress · ✓ complete · → scheduled · ? help needed · × no longer required.

Avoid “sort money” or “fix the house.” Write “record every account balance” or “clear and clean the kitchen worktops.”

Choose the order of attention

  1. Faith, conscience, justice and promises.
  2. Safety and health.
  3. Fixed deadlines and obligations to others.
  4. Basic domestic function: bed, laundry, bathroom, kitchen, food and waste.
  5. Future protection: budgets, safeguards, meal plans and preparation.
  6. Improvement, beauty and optional projects.
Journal exercise two

Use one Action Page for an important matter

Copy only the headings that help. A small matter may need only a next action and date.

  1. What is happening? Facts, dates, amounts, frequency or visible conditions.
  2. What am I assuming? Fear, interpretation or imagined motive that is not established fact.
  3. What is my responsibility? Sin, neglect, duty or practical ownership—without claiming control over another person.
  4. What kind of matter is this? The correct classification points towards the correct remedy.
  5. What would sufficiently resolved look like? Define a stable minimum, not perfection.
  6. What is the next physical action, and when will it happen?
  7. What help, cost, material or journey is needed?
  8. What happened? Record evidence after acting.
  9. Decision: □ complete □ continue □ schedule □ seek help □ defer deliberately.
Before any Rule of Life

Use a suggested daily task list

This is a menu, not a compulsory universal checklist. Copy only the headings suited to your vocation, health, household and current duties.

  • □ Rise and sleep at the intended times.
  • □ Keep the chosen points of prayer.
  • □ Read the day’s Mass readings, where helpful.
  • □ Complete the day’s principal duty.
  • □ Take one action from the Practical Register.
  • □ Complete one necessary domestic action.
  • □ Eat and spend according to the plan.
  • □ Attend to health, movement or rest.
  • □ Make room for charity, relationship or service.
  • □ Take suitable recreation.
  • □ Prepare what tomorrow requires.
  • □ Complete a brief night examination.

Open today’s Mass readings →

Daily readings are an optional support, not an additional burden. Private reading does not replace participation at Mass on Sundays and holy days.

At night, write three short lines

What was completed? What remains but now has a date? What needs to be redirected tomorrow?

Test before formalising

Establish and observe a rhythm

Use the daily list and weekly calendar for at least two to four weeks. Notice what supports prayer, duty, health and charity, and what repeatedly fails because it is badly timed or excessive.

Daily

Fixed prayer, necessary work, food, domestic reset, relationships, recreation and sleep.

Weekly

Mass, lesson review, planning, cleaning, food and money review, rest and service.

Monthly or seasonal

Longer review, maintenance, larger financial decisions and revision after changed circumstances.

A lay use of monastic wisdom

Principles to test in ordinary life

These are inspirations rather than vows. They must be adapted to the duties of a Catholic layperson.

Prayer and recollection

Let prayer mark the day so work, appetite and distraction do not occupy all of it.

Stability

Remain faithful to sound duties and ordinary places rather than continually escaping into novelty.

Work as service

Employment, administration and domestic labour can be performed carefully and peacefully for the good of others.

Moderation

Food, fasting, sleep, study, projects and recreation should be proportionate to health and vocation.

Silence and measured speech

Create space before speaking, messaging or reacting. Silence should serve listening and charity, not punishment or withdrawal.

Stewardship

Care for time, money, food, possessions, body, home and environment as things entrusted to you.

Hospitality and charity

Order should make you more available to legitimate need, not protect a rigid timetable from other people.

Obedience to legitimate duty

Give proper weight to God, Church teaching, lawful authority, employment duties, promises and the responsibilities of your state in life.

Conversion and review

Expect correction. A sound pattern can be revised without drama when evidence or circumstances change.

Recreation and rest

Rest is part of a humane rule, not a prize awarded only after perfection.

Only when enough has been learned

Discern a provisional Rule of Life

Do not write one every day. Draft one after the tested rhythm has supplied evidence—normally after the initial practical period or several weeks of the course.

Date it and call it Provisional Rule of Life — Version 1. Keep it to one or two pages. Use “ordinarily” rather than “always” where illness, charity, travel or legitimate duty may require adaptation.

  1. Purpose and state of life.
  2. Prayer, Mass, sacraments and spiritual reading.
  3. Rising, sleep and health.
  4. Work, travel and study.
  5. Food, fasting and money.
  6. Home, possessions and environment.
  7. Speech, digital conduct and relationships.
  8. Service, recreation and rest.
  9. Weekly review and a larger monthly or seasonal review.

Review after four weeks, after a major change of work or health, or whenever charity and duty show that revision is required. Keep dated versions so development is visible.

Weekly review

Judge the pattern by its fruit

Ticks show completion; discernment asks what the pattern is producing.

  1. Which important duties were consistently completed?
  2. Which practices repeatedly failed, and was the cause unwillingness, timing, excessive scope, health or missing support?
  3. Did the pattern aid prayer, work, home, health, relationships and service?
  4. Did it produce agitation, pride, rigidity or judgement of others?
  5. What should be retained, simplified, moved or removed?
  6. What requires confession, counsel, healthcare or practical assistance?
Final safeguard: a lay Rule of Life remains subordinate to the commandments, the Church, health, charity and the duties of one’s actual vocation. It is a servant of life with God, not a private law for self-condemnation.
