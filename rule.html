A provisional Rule of Life

A Rule of Life is a short, revisable pattern that helps a layperson remember prayer, duty, health, relationships and rest. It is not a monastic vow, a private law or a standard by which to condemn yourself.

Observe before writing

Do not begin by designing an ideal life. Use the Practical page for several weeks. Notice which practices genuinely support fidelity, charity and peace, and which produce rigidity, neglect or collapse.

Principles for an ordered lay life

Prayer gives shape

Fixed points of prayer return the day to God and interrupt appetite, anxiety and self-absorption.

Stability

Remain faithful to sound duties and ordinary places instead of continually escaping into novelty.

Work as service

Employment, study, care, domestic labour and administration can be offered faithfully for the good of others.

Moderation

Food, fasting, sleep, recreation, study and projects should suit health, vocation and charity.

Silence and measured speech

Create space before speaking, messaging or reacting. Silence should serve listening and recollection, not punishment or withdrawal.

Stewardship

Care for time, money, food, body, home and possessions as things entrusted to you.

Community and hospitality

A useful rule leaves room for family, friendship, parish, service and legitimate unexpected need.

Rest and recreation

Rest is part of a human life, not a reward for perfection. Choose forms that restore rather than destabilise duty, money or sleep.

When ready

Write Version 1

Use one or two pages and title them Provisional Rule of Life — Version 1. Date the rule and set a review date about four weeks later.

Suggested headings
  • Purpose: whom this pattern helps you love and serve.
  • Prayer and sacramental life.
  • Rising, sleep and bodily care.
  • Vocation, work, study and travel.
  • Food, fasting and money.
  • Home, possessions and maintenance.
  • Speech, digital conduct and relationships.
  • Community, service, recreation and rest.
  • Weekly and monthly review.

Use words such as ordinarily, normally and where possible. A human rule must allow legitimate adaptation.

Exception clause

Illness, necessary care of another person, travel, legitimate work demands and works of charity may alter the pattern without constituting failure.

Recovery clause

When the pattern breaks down, resume the next fixed prayer, restore food, sleep and essential duties, choose one practical action, and wait for stability before redesigning anything.

Review by its fruits

  1. Does the rule support prayer and the sacraments?
  2. Does it improve ordinary duty, health and responsibility?
  3. Does it make more room for charity and legitimate relationships?
  4. Is any practice unsafe, unrealistic, vain or needlessly severe?
  5. What should be retained, simplified, moved, removed or tested longer?

A Rule of Life remains subordinate to the commandments, Church teaching, health, charity and the real duties of one’s state in life.
